\title{Predictive Planning with Workload-Responsive Correction for Port Gate--Yard Coordination}

\begin{abstract}
Truck appointment systems and predict-then-optimize (PTO) planning can reduce port landside congestion when demand, compliance, and service-capacity models are reliable, but realized gate and yard workloads may diverge from the plan. We study execution-time workload correction in a coupled gate--yard system. At each booking cutoff, a fast rule adjusts only the quota for new appointment confirmations and yard-service capacity; confirmed appointments remain immutable. An optional paired-perturbation tracker moves the operating center. Conditional results explain why gate output propagates congestion downstream, why accurate open-loop PTO is a strong class-restricted benchmark, and why a maintained instruction creates cumulative yard drift when realized gate output persistently exceeds realized yard service. In public-data-calibrated vehicle-level experiments, high-fidelity fixed-pair PTO has the lowest cost and waiting. Under medium and severe model misspecification dominated by service-capacity overestimation, fixed-pair no-reidentification PTO develops persistent congestion that fast-plus-tracker feedback avoids; the archived design has no fast-only arm. A separate documented aggregate reconstruction compares fast-only and fast-plus-tracker variants with a cadence-triggered quota-recovery rule. It imposes the actions and activates the rule after a prespecified delay, rather than estimating capacity or re-solving PTO. Within this reconstruction, fast-only correction has lower diagnostic cost and terminal backlog than fast-plus-tracker feedback, while earlier activation limits post-warm-up persistence after an imposed overloaded pair. The evidence supports predictive planning as the backbone and workload correction as an execution safeguard when workload information arrives before the next planning revision.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Port landside congestion is both an operational and a governance problem. Concentrated truck arrivals lengthen gate queues, make yard service uneven, and transmit delay to drayage operations and nearby road traffic~\citep{GuanLiu2009GateAppointment,TorkjaziHuynhShiri2018TruckTours}. Processing trucks faster at the gate is not sufficient: the yard must be able to absorb the workload released downstream. A gate policy that ignores yard pressure can therefore relocate congestion rather than remove it.

Truck appointment research has developed quotas, time-window designs, collaborative mechanisms, and robust rules for regulating terminal access~\citep{GuanLiu2009GateAppointment,TorkjaziHuynhShiri2018TruckTours,ImYuLee2020TruckAppointmentCooperation,AzabKaramEltawil2017DynamicCollaborativeTAS,LiJiaTaoLin2024RobustGateAppointment}. Terminal-planning research likewise shows the value of integrated and robust resource decisions under uncertain demand and service~\citep{RodriguesAgra2022Survey,AgraRodrigues2022DROBAP,ZhenEtAl2022BerthYard,TanHeWang2017FlexibleYardTemplate}. Landside execution, however, is not a stationary replay of either plan. No-shows, late arrivals, exception trucks, road conditions, staffing, equipment availability, and yard disruptions separate the appointment--capacity plan from the workload actually observed at the gate and yard. The unresolved interface is how a terminal should respond between planning updates, after workload imbalance becomes visible but before the model is re-estimated and the plan is re-solved.

This paper asks when a container terminal should rely on predict-then-optimize (PTO) methods for appointment and capacity planning, and when execution-time workload correction should complement that plan. The conceptual framework distinguishes three operating regimes: executing a predictive plan whose model remains reliable, periodically re-estimating the model and re-solving the plan, and supplementing a maintained plan with workload-responsive correction during execution. The vehicle-level experiments compare maintained fixed-pair planning with workload correction; timely model maintenance is an interpretive boundary rather than an implemented same-error policy. The objective is to characterize these mechanisms, not to construct an automatic mode-selection system.

We develop a two-node gate--yard framework with an explicit decision clock. For each service window, the terminal issues an appointment quota and a yard-capacity instruction; the current request count then determines how many new requests are confirmed, while appointments confirmed for earlier cohorts remain immutable. Confirmed trucks can arrive on time, arrive late, or fail to appear, and gate completions become yard workload. The archived fast correction itself uses the gate queue and the yard queue plus busy yard units; a recent-request mean enters only the appointment-access floor applied after that correction. The policy does not observe the current request count or outstanding appointment pipeline when setting the quota. The previous quota affects the realized switching cost but is not a direct fast-feedback signal. A separable paired-perturbation tracker can move the operating center, but it is not needed to execute the fast correction.

The paper contributes four elements. First, it links appointment confirmation to downstream yard service while distinguishing pending requests, confirmed cohorts, and physical arrivals. Second, it specifies an interpretable workload-correction rule and separates its mandatory baseline, fast response, optional tracker, and safe execution map. Third, it develops conditional mechanism, model-fidelity, and simulation-selection bounds: gate output propagates to the yard, corrective value requires an informative and feasible response direction, and a maintained plan accumulates yard workload only when it induces a persistent realized gate--service imbalance. Fourth, it compares fixed operation, explore-then-select and Monte Carlo-selected fixed-pair PTO implementations, and feedback configurations in public-data-calibrated vehicle-level environments, supplemented by separately implemented diagnostics of component choice and response timing.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:literature-review} reviews related work on truck appointment systems, terminal planning under uncertainty, and queueing feedback control. Section~\ref{sec:system-setting} defines the gate--yard service setting and performance measures. Section~\ref{sec:feedback-coordination} presents the workload-responsive feedback policy. Section~\ref{sec:analytical-properties} develops the analytical properties. Section~\ref{sec:computational-study} reports the public-data-calibrated experiments and separate mechanism diagnostics. Section~\ref{sec:policy-implications} discusses managerial and policy implications. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Literature review}
\label{sec:literature-review}

\subsection{Truck appointment systems, access planning, and compliance}

Truck appointment systems are widely studied as tools for smoothing terminal arrivals and regulating access. Early work showed that quotas and time-window management can reduce peak gate demand~\citep{GuanLiu2009GateAppointment}. Later studies incorporated drayage tours, carrier cooperation, dynamic appointment design, robust decisions, and operational data~\citep{TorkjaziHuynhShiri2018TruckTours,ImYuLee2020TruckAppointmentCooperation,AzabKaramEltawil2017DynamicCollaborativeTAS,LiJiaTaoLin2024RobustGateAppointment,SunShiZheng2025DataDrivenTAS}. This stream establishes appointment confirmation as a port-management decision and motivates the access outcomes reported here: waiting, confirmation, throughput, and unconfirmed requests.

Appointment decisions do not, however, determine the time at which confirmed trucks physically appear. No-shows, late arrivals, exception movements, fleet dispatch choices, and road disruptions separate a confirmed appointment cohort from the realized arrival stream. This timing distinction matters for both model design and policy evaluation: a rule that confirms few requests may report low queues without providing broad access, while a rule based only on current requests may overlook vehicles still arriving from earlier confirmed cohorts.

The appointment literature therefore supplies the upstream decision and the compliance mechanism. It does not by itself resolve how confirmed demand should be coordinated with the yard capacity needed to complete in-terminal service. That downstream planning problem is the focus of the next stream.

\subsection{Predictive gate--yard planning and model maintenance}

Yard and terminal-resource studies establish the downstream side of landside service. Yard templates, yard space, and integrated terminal planning show that internal capacity and resource allocation shape truck service performance~\citep{TanHeWang2017FlexibleYardTemplate,ZhenEtAl2022BerthYard}. Robust and distributionally robust terminal models protect plans against uncertain demand, vessel operations, or service capacity before execution~\citep{RodriguesAgra2022Survey,AgraRodrigues2022DROBAP,LiJiaTaoLin2024RobustGateAppointment}. These studies motivate the paper's gate--yard coupling: appointment confirmation controls upstream workload, whereas yard capacity determines whether gate completions can be absorbed downstream.

Predict-then-optimize uses an estimated outcome model to select operational decisions~\citep{BertsimasKallus2020PrescriptiveAnalytics,ElmachtoubGrigas2022SmartPTO}. In a terminal, that logic appears when forecasts or estimated distributions determine appointment quotas and service resources before execution. Receding-horizon optimization goes further by updating the state or model estimate and repeatedly solving the planning problem~\citep{MayneRawlingsRaoScokaert2000MPC,RawlingsMayneDiehl2017MPC}. The relevant distinction is therefore not simply offline versus online control. It is whether the predictive model remains accurate, whether the operator can re-identify it quickly, and whether queue observations are used between re-solving epochs.

This paper consequently treats PTO as a strong benchmark. An accurate open-loop model should perform well within its decision class, and an adaptive planner can recover from error when the relevant parameter is re-estimated and the planning problem is re-solved quickly. The no-reidentification case is narrower: it maintains an action selected under a belief model even after realized service departs from that belief. Separating these cases distinguishes the experimentally evaluated maintained-plan setting from the interpretation appropriate to adaptive planning.

The closest predictive studies thus provide the appointment--capacity plan, the uncertainty model, and the possibility of re-solving. The remaining interface is execution between planning updates: realized gate output becomes yard input, and current queues may reveal imbalance before a revised predictive model is available.

\subsection{Execution-time workload feedback and the remaining interface}

Queueing control provides a natural language for execution-time correction. Classical studies allow admission, service rates, or scheduling decisions to respond to congestion~\citep{StidhamWeber1989QueueControl,Wein1990ControllableInputs,GeorgeHarrison2001AdjustableService,Neely2010StochasticNetwork}. Stochastic approximation and online queue-learning further motivate low-dimensional action updates from noisy performance observations~\citep{RobbinsMonro1951StochasticApproximation,KushnerYin2003StochasticApproximation,Borkar2008StochasticApproximation,ChenHongLiu2026QueueLearning,CohenSubramanianZhang2024Admission}.

Direct transfer to port landside operations is not immediate. The control concerns advance appointment cohorts rather than only contemporaneous admissions; confirmed trucks can arrive late; gate output becomes yard input; and yard capacity is discrete, shift-constrained, and disrupted. Appointment access must also be reported explicitly because low queues can be produced by confirming fewer requests. Finally, the relevant comparison is a PTO plan that may be difficult to improve upon when its maintained model is accurate.

The three streams therefore enter the paper in sequence. Appointment research defines the confirmed cohort and access consequences; predictive terminal planning links that cohort to downstream capacity and supplies the PTO benchmark; queueing control supplies an execution-time response to observed imbalance. This paper combines these elements to organize the distinction among maintained prediction, timely model maintenance, and workload correction in a coupled gate--yard system. Its vehicle-level experiments compare the maintained-plan and correction cases; a separate aggregate diagnostic examines recovery timing under a prespecified quota-recovery rule rather than implementing model re-identification.

The proposed correction layer is deliberately narrower than a terminal optimizer, yard-crane scheduler, or automatic mode-selection system. It adjusts two observable levers while detailed planning remains outside the layer. Table~\ref{tab:literature-gap-map} summarizes how the three streams define the interface studied here.

\section{System setting and operational decisions}
\label{sec:system-setting}

This section translates the port-management problem into a sequence of operational events. For each service window, the policy first issues an appointment quota and activates yard capacity. The booking system then applies that quota to the realized current request count. The quota applies only to the new cohort; a request confirmed under it cannot subsequently be withdrawn by the feedback policy. Confirmed trucks may appear on time, appear late, or fail to appear; the gate processes realized arrivals; and each gate completion creates yard workload. We distinguish the issued quota \(q_t\) from the resulting confirmation count \(a_t\) throughout.

\subsection{Port landside service ecosystem and decision timing}
\label{subsec:3-1-port-landside-service-ecosystem-and-decision-timing}

We consider the landside service ecosystem of a container terminal in which truck access decisions, gate processing, and yard operations are operationally coupled. Trucking companies submit requests for a specified service window before its booking cutoff. At that cutoff, the terminal confirms a subset of the still-pending requests. The confirmation decision applies only to the new cohort: appointments confirmed at earlier cutoffs remain valid and enter the outstanding pipeline until the truck appears or exhausts its lateness allowance. Confirmed trucks may arrive on time, arrive late, or fail to appear; trucks that complete gate processing subsequently require yard service. Existing truck-appointment studies show how time-window and confirmation decisions can regulate terminal arrivals~\citep{GuanLiu2009GateAppointment,TorkjaziHuynhShiri2018TruckTours,LiJiaTaoLin2024RobustGateAppointment}. The terminal in our setting faces an additional coordination task: it must match newly confirmed upstream workload with sufficient downstream capacity.

The base model places the booking cutoff immediately before the associated service window. This convention lets the state at the cutoff inform both the new confirmations and the capacity activated for that window. At terminals that close appointment books earlier, the same decision applies to the next service cohort whose book remains open; it does not authorize cancellation of a closed cohort. Longer confirmation lead times would introduce an explicit action delay and are outside the present computational design. We therefore treat the truck appointment system as one control component within a broader port service ecosystem rather than as an isolated slot-allocation problem.

Figure~\ref{fig:service-ecosystem} summarizes the decision sequence. Predictive planning sets the operating backbone before or between operating blocks, while the feedback layer uses realized gate and yard states to correct deviations during execution. The figure is a system schematic, not an empirical process map.

The operating horizon is divided into discrete decision windows indexed by

\[
t=1,\ldots,T.
\]

The window length should be chosen for the operational response being modeled. The computational study uses 30-minute windows to resolve intraday queue responses; this is a simulation design choice rather than a resolution inferred from the daily and weekly public records. At the beginning of window \(t\), the policy selects the instruction

\[
\theta_t=(q_t,\mu_t^y),
\]

where \(q_t\) is the integer appointment-quota instruction for the pending cohort and \(\mu_t^y\) is an aggregate yard-service capacity level activated for that window. With \(R_t\) pending requests, the number newly confirmed is the mechanical outcome

\[
a_t=\min\{R_t,q_t\}.
\]

Neither \(q_t\) nor \(a_t\) cancels or reschedules a request already contained in the outstanding pipeline. The capacity variable may represent the number of active service units, a staffing--equipment package, or another implementable capacity tier. The two controls regulate newly confirmed workload and the capacity available to complete downstream service. We reserve \emph{quota} for the issued instruction, \emph{confirmation} for \(a_t\), and \emph{physical arrival} and \emph{completion} for later events.

Let \(R_t\) denote the current appointment requests to which the issued quota is applied. Let \(L\) be the maximum number of windows by which a confirmed truck remains eligible to arrive late, and let \(H_t=(H_{t,1},\ldots,H_{t,L})\) denote the simulator's appointment pipeline. Component \(H_{t,d}\) records the still-eligible, not-yet-appeared requests confirmed for cohort \(t-d\); thus \(d\) is cohort age at the current cutoff, not the number of lateness windows remaining. The base simulation sets \(L=2\); this is a finite-support design assumption, not a rule inferred from the public aggregate data. For a more general analytical model, \(C_t\), \(O_t\), \(M_t\), and \(E_t\) can represent compliance information, container-yard occupancy excluding the truck service queue, equipment and service conditions, and exogenous disruptions.

The vehicle-level simulation also retains service already in progress. Let \(B_t^g\) and \(B_t^y\) be the gate- and yard-server status vectors at the cutoff, including the remaining service time of each busy server, and let \(I_t^g\) and \(I_t^y\) be the corresponding numbers of busy servers. A general analytical state is

\[
\Xi_t=(Q_t^g,Q_t^y,B_t^g,B_t^y,H_t,C_t,O_t,M_t,E_t,\theta_{t-1}).
\]

The archived simulator internally maintains the queues, server records, pending appearances, request history, and previous instruction needed to advance the event process. The general framework allows a controller to use a history \(\mathcal I_t\), but the archived fast rule reads only

\[
S_t^{\mathrm{fast}}=
(Q_t^g,Q_t^y,I_t^y,\overline R_t^{(6)}).
\]

Here \(Q_t^g\) and \(Q_t^y\) are the numbers waiting at the gate and yard, \(I_t^y\) is the number of busy yard units, and \(\overline R_t^{(6)}\) is the available mean over at most six preceding request counts, initialized at 25. The workload correction in Eqs.~(11)--(16) uses only \(Q_t^g\) and \(Q_t^y+I_t^y\); \(\overline R_t^{(6)}\) enters only the appointment-access floor in Eq.~(5). The current request count \(R_t\), pipeline \(H_t\), gate-busy count, recent-arrival summary, previous instruction, and current disruption realization are not direct workload-correction inputs. After the quota is issued, \(R_t\) truncates confirmations mechanically, the simulator advances \(H_t\), and a disruption can reduce effective yard capacity. The previous quota enters the realized switching cost observed by the optional tracker. The event process evolves on the archived simulator state; \(S_t^{\mathrm{fast}}\) is the complete input tuple for the executed fast policy, but it is not asserted to be Markov-sufficient for the event process.

All policies must satisfy the physical feasible set

\[
\theta_t\in\Theta_t^{\mathrm{phys}},
\]

with bounds

\[
\underline q_t\le q_t\le\overline q_t,
\qquad
\underline\mu_t^y\le\mu_t^y\le\overline\mu_t^y.
\]

The quota bounds define the smallest and largest instructions that the appointment system can issue; current request availability enters only through \(a_t=\min\{R_t,q_t\}\), so confirmations cannot exceed pending requests. The physical set can also include shift or equipment availability constraints and limits on abrupt changes between adjacent windows. It contains no operation that removes an appointment from \(H_t\). The feedback policy later adds an appointment-access constraint, producing a policy-specific subset \(\Theta_t^{\mathrm{FB}}\subseteq\Theta_t^{\mathrm{phys}}\), where the superscript \(\mathrm{FB}\) denotes feedback; the fixed and PTO benchmarks are evaluated within their stated candidate sets under the physical bounds. When yard capacity must be expressed as an integer number of active units, we write

\[
m_t\in\{\underline m_t,\underline m_t+1,\ldots,\overline m_t\},
\qquad
\mu_t^y=h(m_t),
\]

where \(\underline m_t\) and \(\overline m_t\) are the available lower and upper unit limits and \(h(\cdot)\) maps equipment or staffing units into aggregate service capacity. The analytical model can use a continuous relaxation, while an implementation must map the relaxed instruction to a feasible integer level using a pre-specified deterministic tie-breaking rule.

The scope is a landside coordination layer. The model does not attempt to reproduce berth allocation, detailed container-stacking decisions, individual crane dispatch, external road-network routing, or microscopic truck trajectories. Its purpose is narrower: to examine how new appointment confirmation and yard-service capacity interact when realized workload deviates from a planned path.

\subsection{Gate--yard tandem dynamics with appointment compliance}
\label{subsec:3-2-gate-yard-tandem-dynamics-with-appointment-compliance}

Appointment confirmation does not uniquely determine the number or timing of trucks that arrive. To make the timing explicit, let \(X_{\tau,d}\) denote the number of requests confirmed for cohort \(\tau\) that physically arrive \(d\in\{0,\ldots,L\}\) windows later; the remaining confirmed requests in the cohort are no-shows. Let \(A_t^e\) collect walk-ins and other exception arrivals. The physical arrivals in window \(t\) are therefore

\[
A_t=\sum_{d=0}^{L}X_{t-d,d}+A_t^e.
\]

To make the appointment memory explicit, let \(P_{\tau,t}\) be the number of requests confirmed for cohort \(\tau\) that have not appeared by the cutoff of window \(t\) and remain eligible to arrive. Then \(H_t=(P_{t-1,t},\ldots,P_{t-L,t})\). After arrivals in window \(t\), the new cohort enters the pipeline as \(P_{t,t+1}=a_t-X_{t,0}\). For an earlier cohort with \(1\le t-\tau<L\), \(P_{\tau,t+1}=P_{\tau,t}-X_{\tau,t-\tau}\); after its final eligible window, any residual is removed as a no-show. With 30-minute windows, the base value \(L=2\) corresponds to a maximum modeled lateness of 60 minutes. The computational study separately checks one- and four-window supports because the aggregate public sources identify missed reservations but not the intraday distribution of lateness. Equivalently, the aggregate arrival kernel can be written as

\[
A_t=A_t(a_t,H_t,C_t,E_t,\varepsilon_t),
\]

where \(\varepsilon_t\) captures residual uncertainty. An equivalent conditional-moment representation is

\[
\mathbb E[A_t\mid a_t,H_t,C_t,E_t]
=\lambda_t(a_t,H_t,C_t,E_t),
\]

and

\[
\operatorname{Var}(A_t\mid a_t,H_t,C_t,E_t)
=\sigma_t^2(a_t,H_t,C_t,E_t)>0.
\]

Thus, current arrivals depend on both the new confirmation decision and unresolved earlier cohorts, and they remain stochastic after those quantities are observed. The base simulation assigns confirmed trucks to on-time, one-window-late, two-window-late, or no-show outcomes and adds exception arrivals independently. This construction distinguishes pending requests, newly confirmed cohorts, immutable outstanding appointments, and physical arrivals.

Let \(N_t^g=Q_t^g+I_t^g\) and \(N_t^y=Q_t^y+I_t^y\) denote the total unfinished gate and yard workloads, including trucks waiting or in service. To distinguish potential service from completed service, let

\[
G_t=G_t(B_t^g,M_t,E_t)
\]

be the potential number of trucks that the gate can process during window \(t\), before losses caused by within-window idleness or late arrivals are accounted for. Let \(D_t^g\) be the actual number of gate completions. In a vehicle-level system,

\[
0\le D_t^g\le\min\{N_t^g+A_t,G_t\}.
\]

Similarly, let

\[
Y_t(\mu_t^y)=Y_t(\mu_t^y,B_t^y,M_t,O_t,E_t)
\]

be potential yard-service capacity under the chosen capacity level, and let \(D_t^y\) be actual yard completions. Then

\[
0\le D_t^y\le\min\{N_t^y+D_t^g,Y_t(\mu_t^y)\}.
\]

The inequalities separate available work, potential capacity, and realized completion. They become equalities in an aggregate saturation model, or when work is available early enough to keep the relevant servers busy throughout the usable part of the window. In a vehicle-level discrete-event system, a truck arriving near the end of a window cannot recover capacity lost while a server was idle. Disruptions can further reduce potential yard capacity even when the planned capacity \(\mu_t^y\) is unchanged.

The exact count balance for unfinished gate workload is

\[
N_{t+1}^g
=N_t^g+A_t-D_t^g.
\tag{1}
\]

The corresponding yard balance is

\[
N_{t+1}^y
=N_t^y+D_t^g-D_t^y.
\tag{2}
\]

Equations~(1)--(2) are mass-balance identities and reveal the central operational coupling. The gate output \(D_t^g\) is the yard input. Increasing appointment confirmation can raise gate workload, while accelerating gate processing can shift trucks downstream more quickly. If realized yard service is not adjusted accordingly, an apparent improvement at the gate can coexist with persistent or increasing unfinished workload inside the terminal. Gate and yard performance must therefore be evaluated jointly.

The archived vehicle-level implementation is a first-come-first-served discrete-event simulator. Within each one-minute event step, newly appearing trucks join the gate queue, enter an available gate server in arrival order, move to the yard queue after gate completion, and then enter an available active yard server. Service that is unfinished at a decision-window boundary remains in progress, and gate- and yard-queue areas are accumulated minute by minute. The supplied reproduction archive contains the simulator source, experiment configurations, deterministic seed rules, and the raw replication outputs used for the three vehicle-level policy experiments and the high-fidelity component diagnostic. The documented independent aggregate reconstructions used for Tables~\ref{tab:aggregate-recovery} and~\ref{tab:fast-feedback-robustness} instead use aggregate queue and capacity approximations; their absolute values are not pooled with the vehicle-level model-fidelity results.

\subsection{Performance measures, appointment-access protection, and capacity scope}
\label{subsec:3-3-performance-measures-access-protection-and-capacity-scope}

The terminal balances congestion, resource use, operational adjustment, and appointment access. The archived vehicle-level implementation distinguishes the appointment-quota instruction from realized confirmations. Let \(q_t\in\{12,\ldots,30\}\) be the integer quota returned by the policy. The number of newly confirmed requests and the number left unconfirmed are

\[
a_t=\min\{R_t,q_t\},
\qquad
U_t^{\mathrm{unc}}=R_t-a_t.
\]

The cost uses the unconfirmed share

\[
r_t^U=\frac{U_t^{\mathrm{unc}}}{\max\{R_t,1\}},
\]

rather than the raw count \(U_t^{\mathrm{unc}}\). Let \(\widetilde m_t\) denote the effective number of active yard units after the realized capacity disruption in window \(t\), and let \(\mathcal Q_t^g\) and \(\mathcal Q_t^y\) be the time-average waiting-queue lengths accumulated minute by minute during the window. The implemented congestion-and-access score is

\[
V_t=
\mathbf 1
\left\{
\mathcal Q_t^g>20
\ \text{or}\
\mathcal Q_t^y>20
\right\}
+5r_t^U.
\]

It combines a congestion-threshold indicator with appointment-access loss; it is not a count of trucks that fail to complete service. Let \(\omega_t\) collect the within-window appearance, service, exception-arrival, and disruption realizations. The dimensionless additive one-period cost implemented in the vehicle-level experiments is

\[
\begin{aligned}
c_t(\Xi_t,\theta_t,\omega_t)
=\;&
w_g\frac{\mathcal Q_t^g}{20}
+w_y\frac{\mathcal Q_t^y}{20}
+w_r\frac{\widetilde m_t}{10}\\
&+w_s\frac{|q_t-q_{t-1}|}{18}
+w_v V_t .
\end{aligned}
\tag{3}
\]

The divisors 20, 10, and 18 are, respectively, the queue reference, the maximum number of active yard units, and the appointment-quota span. The archived implementation has exactly these five weighted components. It penalizes quota changes but contains neither a separate yard-capacity-change penalty nor a container-yard-occupancy term. Legacy output files use the label ``rejected requests'' for \(U_t^{\mathrm{unc}}\). These cohort-specific requests exit the current decision; any later resubmission appears as a new request in a future \(R_{t'}\). The variable does not include confirmed trucks that arrive late, remain in a queue, or fail to appear.

Let \(t_{\mathrm w}\) be the number of warm-up windows excluded from evaluation. The expected post-warm-up average cost of a policy \(\pi\) under the true operating environment \(\mathcal M\) is

\[
J_{\mathcal M}(\pi)
=\mathbb E_{\mathcal M,\pi}
\left[
\frac{1}{T-t_{\mathrm w}}
\sum_{t=t_{\mathrm w}+1}^{T}c_t(\Xi_t,\theta_t,\omega_t)
\right].
\tag{4}
\]

Let \(\Pi^{\mathrm{phys}}\) be the class of nonanticipative policies satisfying \(\theta_t=\pi_t(\mathcal I_t)\in\Theta_t^{\mathrm{phys}}\). The common decision problem underlying the policy comparisons is

\[
\inf_{\pi\in\Pi^{\mathrm{phys}}}J_{\mathcal M}(\pi).
\tag{4a}
\]

The paper does not claim to solve Eq.~(4a) over all admissible policies. The fixed baseline, predictive-planning policies, and workload-feedback policies studied below are restricted policy families with different information and updating structures. Writing the common problem explicitly ensures that their costs refer to the same gate--yard system and performance accounting.

Equation~(3) is a normalized performance index rather than a financial or social-welfare measure. The computational study uses \(t_{\mathrm w}=7\times48\) windows and reports the post-warm-up average in Eq.~(4), rather than a 30-day total. The archived source specifies all vehicle-level normalization maps, weights, and component calculations, so the reported vehicle-level weighted costs can be regenerated from the deposited configurations and raw outputs. Confirmation, throughput, unconfirmed requests, and resource hours per completed truck are also reported separately so that a low weighted cost cannot conceal reduced appointment access or additional resource use. The independent aggregate reconstructions use their separately documented diagnostic cost and are not pooled with the vehicle-level cost values.

The appointment-access safeguard can be imposed either as a penalty or as a constraint. In the computational feedback policy, the executed quota is subject to the rolling floor

\[
q_t\ge \underline q_t^{\mathrm{acc}}
:=\max\left\{\underline q_t,
\min\left\{\overline q_t,
\left\lceil\rho_{\min}\overline R_t\right\rceil
\right\}\right\}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
a_t\ge\min\{R_t,\underline q_t^{\mathrm{acc}}\},
\tag{5}
\]

where \(\rho_{\min}\in(0,1]\) is a target confirmation fraction and \(\overline R_t\) is computed from a pre-specified lookback \(W_R\): \(\overline R_t=|\mathcal W_t|^{-1}\sum_{s\in\mathcal W_t}R_s\), with \(\mathcal W_t=\{\max(1,t-W_R),\ldots,t-1\}\) and a pre-specified initial mean when \(\mathcal W_t\) is empty. In the vehicle-level implementation, \(W_R=6\) decision windows; when no request history is yet available, the request mean is initialized at 25 requests per window. The nested bounds ensure that the quota floor remains physically feasible; current requests enter only afterward through \(a_t=\min\{R_t,q_t\}\). We write \(\Theta_t^{\mathrm{FB}}\) for the subset of \(\Theta_t^{\mathrm{phys}}\) satisfying the quota floor in Eq.~(5). This rolling floor is specific to feedback. The explore-then-select PTO surrogate separately filters out fixed quotas below 85\% of its estimated mean requests, whereas the model-fidelity Monte Carlo candidate class has no access floor. Equation~(5) protects the opportunity to receive an appointment, not physical arrival, service completion, or a waiting-time target.

Mean and tail waiting measures are retained as evaluation outcomes. For a waiting-time random variable \(W\), the 95th percentile (P95) is

\[
\operatorname{P95}(W)
=\inf\{z:\Pr(W\le z)\ge0.95\}.
\tag{6}
\]

P95 waiting is not inserted as a primitive one-period cost because it is a path- or sample-level statistic rather than a naturally additive state cost. If tail congestion is to be optimized directly, an additive exceedance penalty such as \([Q_t-\kappa]_+\), or a formally defined conditional value-at-risk term, can be used. The computational study reports mean and P95 waiting, queue levels, throughput, confirmation, unconfirmed requests, utilization, and weighted performance cost.

Finally, we use a statewise capacity-slack condition to delimit an operating region in which corrective authority exists. Let \(\mathcal X_0\) denote the analytical state region over which this condition is asserted, and write the physical feasible set as \(\Theta_t^{\mathrm{phys}}(\xi)\) when it is state dependent. The condition is: there are constants \(\delta_g,\delta_y>0\) such that, for every \(\xi\in\mathcal X_0\), there exists one action \(\theta(\xi)=(q(\xi),\mu^y(\xi))\in\Theta_t^{\mathrm{phys}}(\xi)\) that satisfies both

\[
\mathbb E[A_t\mid \Xi_t=\xi,\theta_t=\theta(\xi)]
\le \mathbb E[G_t\mid \Xi_t=\xi]-\delta_g,
\tag{7}
\]

and

\[
\mathbb E[D_t^g\mid \Xi_t=\xi,\theta_t=\theta(\xi)]
\le \mathbb E[Y_t(\mu^y(\xi))\mid \Xi_t=\xi,\theta_t=\theta(\xi)]-\delta_y.
\tag{8}
\]

The quantifier order is therefore \(\forall\xi\in\mathcal X_0\,\exists\theta(\xi)\), not the stronger requirement that one fixed action work for every state. Equations~(7)--(8) define the region in which a corrective action is physically available; the drift results below still require the evaluated policy to choose an action that actually improves the realized input--service imbalance. The condition is not derived from the feedback rule and is neither used as, nor sufficient for, a stochastic-stability theorem. Persistent failure for every feasible action indicates structural capacity shortage rather than an algorithmic failure.

\section{Workload-responsive feedback coordination}
\label{sec:feedback-coordination}

The coordination mechanism separates three operations that a reader must not conflate: choosing a physical baseline, applying a per-window fast correction, and optionally tracking a moving operating center. The baseline may come from an offline predictive plan, a documented operating rule, or another independently chosen feasible pair. The fast rule then moves the appointment quota and yard-capacity instruction when observed workload reveals an imbalance. The optional paired-perturbation tracker changes the center from sequential realized costs; it is not required to execute workload correction. The primary vehicle-level high-fidelity and model-misspecification comparisons evaluate the combined fast-plus-tracker implementation. A high-fidelity component comparison and an independent aggregate severe-error reconstruction separately evaluate the tracker's incremental effect.

\subsection{Policy families and information structure}
\label{subsec:4-1-policy-class-and-information-structure}

We organize the evaluated policies into three families that represent different uses of operational information. A policy first issues an appointment--capacity instruction and then passes it through a physical execution map \(\Gamma_t^{\mathrm{phys}}\), which clips the quota and capacity to their available levels and applies pre-specified integer rounding. Current request availability then produces \(a_t=\min\{R_t,q_t\}\). This map does not impose the feedback-specific access floor. The non-responsive family is represented by a fixed instruction

\[
\theta_t=\Gamma_t^{\mathrm{phys}}(\vartheta^F),
\qquad \vartheta^F=(q^F,\mu^{y,F}),
\qquad t=1,\ldots,T,
\tag{9}
\]

or, more generally, a predetermined intraday instruction pattern that is not revised in response to realized queues. The realized confirmation count can still fall below \(q^F\) when fewer requests are available; this mechanical mapping is not state feedback.

The predictive-planning family is anchored by predict-then-optimize (PTO), which serves as the strong planning benchmark. PTO follows the predictive-to-prescriptive logic of selecting a decision through an estimated model of uncertain outcomes~\citep{BertsimasKallus2020PrescriptiveAnalytics,ElmachtoubGrigas2022SmartPTO}. A planning implementation selects an appointment--capacity plan from an outcome model and then executes the resulting instruction without queue feedback. In the model-fidelity stress test, the selected pair is maintained throughout the evaluation horizon without re-estimating capacity from realized completions. We call this experimental benchmark \emph{fixed-pair no-reidentification PTO}. Let \(\vartheta_t=(q_t,\mu_t^y)\) denote a planned appointment quota and yard-capacity instruction, and let

\[
u=(\vartheta_1,\ldots,\vartheta_T)\in\mathcal U
\]

be an open-loop appointment-capacity plan. Physical bounds induce the instruction map

\[
\chi(\vartheta_t)
=\Gamma_t^{\mathrm{phys}}(\vartheta_t)
=\theta_t.
\]

This mechanical execution makes the issued instruction physically feasible without using gate or yard queues and without imposing the feedback-specific access floor. The state variable \(R_t\) then determines the realized confirmation count. PTO uses a belief model \(\widehat{\mathcal M}\) for demand, compliance, service times, and capacity, and solves

\[
u^{\mathrm{PTO}}
\in\arg\min_{u\in\mathcal U}\widehat J(u),
\qquad
\widehat J(u)=
\mathbb E_{\widehat{\mathcal M}}
\left[
\frac{1}{T-t_{\mathrm w}}
\sum_{t=t_{\mathrm w}+1}^{T}
c_t\bigl(\Xi_t,\chi(\vartheta_t),\omega_t\bigr)
\right].
\tag{10}
\]

The general plan class in Eq.~(10) may vary across the day, but it is open-loop because execution is not revised using realized gate and yard queues. The computational study uses the narrower finite subclass

\[
\mathcal U_{\mathrm{pair}}
=\left\{u_{\vartheta}=(\vartheta,\ldots,\vartheta):
\vartheta\in\Theta^{\mathrm{cand}}\right\}
\subseteq\mathcal U,
\]

where \(\vartheta=(q,\mu^y)\) specifies one intended appointment quota and yard-capacity level and \(\Theta^{\mathrm{cand}}\) is a finite subset of the physical instruction set. The vehicle-level study uses two distinct selectors within this class. The high-fidelity comparison, access study, and component study use an explore-then-select implementation: during the first 20\% of the 30-day run, the quota spans 12--30 and the yard level cycles through 6--10; the resulting request and appearance summaries parameterize a deterministic queueing surrogate that scores the eligible subset of the 95-pair grid after filtering quotas below 85\% of estimated mean requests. The selected pair is then maintained unless a rolling-surrogate variant is enabled. The six-day exploration lies inside the seven-day warm-up, so the reported metrics exclude it, although the simulated state carries through to evaluation. The component run also contains a 24-hour update variant using a trailing 144-window record and a three-hour update variant using a trailing 48-window record. These variants refresh request and appearance summaries and rerank the surrogate; they do not re-identify gate or yard service capacity.

The model-fidelity experiment instead evaluates every pair by four Monte Carlo planning paths under each fidelity-specific belief model, selects the lowest estimated-cost pair before policy evaluation, and then maintains that pair. Equation~(28a) applies to this selector, not to the explore-then-select or rolling-surrogate implementations. The separate aggregate recovery diagnostic introduced in Section~\ref{subsec:6-4-pto-model-fidelity-and-the-value-of-feedback-correction} uses neither selector: it imposes an overloaded pair, an always-on fixed-pair reference, and a state-dependent quota-recovery rule.

The workload-feedback family is anchored by fast workload-responsive correction. It does not reconstruct the full joint distribution of arrivals, compliance, and service before acting. Instead, it shifts the appointment quota and yard capacity when observed queues indicate an accumulated flow imbalance. Its instruction is passed through a feedback execution map \(\Gamma_t^{\mathrm{FB}}\), which applies the physical map and then enforces Eq.~(5), so the resulting instruction belongs to \(\Theta_t^{\mathrm{FB}}\). This policy uses more execution-time information than fixed-pair no-reidentification PTO because it observes realized queue states, and it operates under an additional access constraint. It therefore requires observable workload signals, meaningful feedback signs, feasible corrective authority, and sufficient capacity for corrective actions to matter. The paired-perturbation tracker introduced later changes the operating center but is not part of the core definition.

This information structure permits a documented plan or operating rule to supply the physical baseline while queue feedback corrects departures from it. In the computational study, the vehicle-level predictive-planning variants isolate model fidelity within the implemented fixed-pair candidate class. The separate aggregate diagnostic examines the delay before a prespecified action-revision rule becomes available; it does not represent model estimation or re-optimization. The feedback variants isolate whether the optional tracker is enabled.

\subsection{Workload-level feedback design}
\label{subsec:4-2-workload-level-feedback-design}

The fast layer answers an immediate operational question: how should the terminal move the current-window instruction when the realized system is no longer following the planned path? Workload levels retain accumulated imbalance that a single-window flow can hide. The gate signal uses the waiting queue \(Q_t^g\), while the yard signal uses total unfinished workload \(N_t^y=Q_t^y+I_t^y\):

\[
p_t^g=\operatorname{clip}\left(\frac{Q_t^g}{\kappa_g},0,\overline p_g\right),
\tag{11}
\]

and

\[
p_t^y=\operatorname{clip}\left(
\frac{Q_t^y+I_t^y}{\kappa_y},0,\overline p_y
\right),
\tag{12}
\]

where \(I_t^y\) is the number of trucks currently in yard service. In the implemented one-truck-per-unit service model, it equals the number of busy yard units, so \(Q_t^y+I_t^y\) has the physical meaning ``trucks waiting or in service.'' The constants \(\kappa_g\) and \(\kappa_y\) scale the two signals, and clipping prevents an extreme observation from generating an unbounded action change. The diagnostic feedback vector is

\[
F_t=
\begin{pmatrix}
p_t^g\\
p_t^y
\end{pmatrix}.
\tag{13}
\]

Let \(z^0=(z^{0,q},z^{0,y})\in[0,1]^2\) be an externally specified normalized operating center whose coordinates index the appointment-quota and yard-capacity ranges. The quota coordinate is mapped over \([\underline q,\overline q]\); realized confirmations remain \(a_t=\min\{R_t,q_t\}\). The center is a required policy input rather than an output of fast correction. An implementation may obtain it from a maintained predictive plan, a standard operating rule, or another documented baseline; its source and numerical value are part of the policy specification. The corresponding nominal physical instruction is

\[
\Psi(z)=
\begin{pmatrix}
\underline q+z^q(\overline q-\underline q)\\
\underline\mu^y+z^y(\overline\mu^y-\underline\mu^y)
\end{pmatrix}.
\tag{14}
\]

A workload correction of the form

\[
b(F_t)=
\begin{pmatrix}
-k_{gg}p_t^g-k_{gy}p_t^y\\
k_{yg}p_t^g+k_{yy}(p_t^y-p_y^0)
\end{pmatrix}
\tag{15}
\]

implements the desired operational signs. Gate and yard pressure both reduce the normalized appointment component. Yard pressure raises the normalized yard-capacity component, whereas low yard pressure allows capacity to return toward a less resource-intensive level. Gate pressure can also raise yard capacity because clearing the gate will soon generate downstream work.

The numerical implementation uses \(\kappa_g=\kappa_y=20\), \(\overline p_g=\overline p_y=1.5\), and

\[
k_{gg}=0.18,\quad
k_{gy}=0.28,\quad
k_{yg}=0.08,\quad
k_{yy}=0.45,\quad
p_y^0=0.20.
\tag{16}
\]

These coefficients are design gains rather than estimates of a physical law. Their purpose is to encode a transparent feedback direction. The appointment response is stronger to yard pressure than to gate pressure because downstream congestion cannot be removed by gate processing alone, while yard capacity rises above a low workload reference. The present evidence does not identify an optimal gain vector. A separate queue-only reference diagnostic scales the gains between 0.5 and 1.5; it is a limited design check rather than validation of Eq.~(12) or terminal-specific calibration.

Appointment-access protection is not introduced as a third control. The implemented action vector remains two-dimensional. Access is represented by the floor in Eq.~(5), the unconfirmed-request share inside the congestion-and-access score \(V_t\) in Eq.~(3), and separate reporting of confirmation, throughput, and unconfirmed requests. These devices protect the opportunity to obtain an appointment; they do not impose a completion or waiting-time guarantee.

The core state-responsive instruction is \(\Psi(z^0+b(F_t))\); \(\Gamma_t^{\mathrm{FB}}\) then applies physical truncation, integer rounding, and the appointment-access floor. The normalized operating center \(z^0\) is an externally supplied baseline input rather than an estimate inferred from \(F_t\), while \(b(F_t)\) supplies the execution-time correction. The optional tracker below replaces \(z^0\) by an updated sequence \(z_n\).

\subsection{Optional sequential paired-perturbation tracker and safe execution}
\label{subsec:4-3-paired-perturbation-adaptation-and-safe-execution}

The optional paired-perturbation tracker asks which normalized operating center the fast correction should use. Starting from the documented input \(z_0=z^0\), it updates the center from two sequential one-window performance observations. This target is not the remaining-horizon objective in Eq.~(4): it is the realized window cost observed after each perturbed action. Because the two actions are executed in consecutive windows rather than from cloned system states, their cost difference is a directional adaptation score, not an unbiased central finite-difference gradient of a fixed objective. Appointment consequences can also extend beyond the observation window through late arrivals and subsequent queues.

At iteration \(n\), the policy selects coordinate \(i_n\in\{1,2\}\). Let \(e_{i_n}\) denote the associated unit vector, let \(s\in\{-1,+1\}\), and let \(\delta_n>0\) be the perturbation radius. A normalized candidate is

\[
\widetilde z_{n,s,t}
=z_n+s\delta_ne_{i_n}+b(F_t).
\tag{17}
\]

The operational action is

\[
\theta_{n,s,t}
=\Gamma_t^{\mathrm{FB}}
\left(\Psi\left(\Pi_{[0,1]^2}(\widetilde z_{n,s,t})\right)\right),
\tag{18}
\]

where \(\Pi_{[0,1]^2}\) clips the normalized candidate and \(\Gamma_t^{\mathrm{FB}}\) supplies the safe execution map. Let \(\ell_{n,-}\) and \(\ell_{n,+}\) be the realized weighted costs accumulated during the two consecutive decision windows after the negative and positive actions are implemented. The signed paired-perturbation score is

\[
\widehat d_n
=\frac{\ell_{n,+}-\ell_{n,-}}{2\delta_n}e_{i_n}.
\tag{19}
\]

The normalization by \(2\delta_n\) records the intended separation before clipping and the integer execution mapping. It does not restore a gradient interpretation when the executable actions have unequal spacing or coincide. The score can also contain state-evolution, fixed-order, adjustment-cost, and fast-feedback effects because \(S_t^{\mathrm{fast}}\) and \(F_t\) change between the two windows. These effects are treated as implementation bias rather than as zero-mean sampling noise.

The normalized center is updated according to

\[
z_{n+1}
=\Pi_{[0,1]^2}
\left(z_n-\eta_n\widehat d_n\right),
\tag{20}
\]

where \(\eta_n\) is a diminishing learning rate. The implemented schedules are

\[
\eta_n=\eta_0(n+1)^{-0.6}
\tag{21}
\]

and

\[
\delta_n=\max\{\delta_{\min},\delta_0(n+1)^{-1/3}\}.
\tag{22}
\]

The positive perturbation floor \(\delta_{\min}\) prevents the nominal perturbation from vanishing. It may reduce, but cannot eliminate, coincident executed actions, especially for the discrete capacity coordinate. The archived vehicle-level implementation fully specifies the tracker execution sequence. At each policy reset, the center is initialized from the physical pair \((25,8)\), and the first perturbed coordinate is drawn from the pseudo-random-number generator initialized by the recorded policy seed. Every pair is executed in negative--positive order, the center is updated after the positive-window cost is observed, and the next coordinate is then drawn from the same generator. The update operates from the beginning of the simulation; there is no separate warm-up update rule. Clipping, projection, and deterministic integer rounding are applied to every executed action, and pairs whose physical actions coincide after this mapping are retained rather than discarded. These specifications make the reported tracker instance reproducible, but they do not establish convergence of \(\{z_n\}\), stabilization of the integer actions, or closed-loop queue stability.

When the optional tracker is enabled, its operational procedure is as follows.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt,topsep=3pt]
\item For the archived vehicle-level experiments, initialize from \(\theta^0=(25,8)\), so that \(z_0=z^0=(13/18,1/2)\).
\item Read the gate queue, yard queue, busy-yard count, and mean of up to six preceding request counts. The tracker additionally retains its center, coordinate, perturbation sign, and stored negative-perturbation cost as internal policy state.
\item Compute the fast workload correction in Eq.~(15).
\item Perturb one coordinate of the normalized operating center, convert the candidate to a physical action, and enforce the safe feasible set.
\item Observe the realized one-window cost, apply the opposite perturbation in the next window, and observe the second cost.
\item Form the signed score in Eq.~(19), update the center using Eq.~(20), and select the next coordinate.
\item Repeat while reporting waiting, throughput, confirmation, resource use, and tail congestion separately.
\end{enumerate}

The update is motivated by two-point stochastic approximation and online queue learning~\citep{RobbinsMonro1951StochasticApproximation,KushnerYin2003StochasticApproximation,ChenHongLiu2026QueueLearning}, but its sequential implementation does not satisfy the standard same-objective central-difference construction. Its operational role is narrower: it perturbs a low-dimensional baseline while the fast layer responds directly to workload and the execution map enforces feasible appointment and integer-capacity actions.

The methodological contribution consequently lies in the coupled gate--yard correction architecture rather than in a new finite-difference convergence result. The confirmation decision changes a future arrival pipeline, gate completions feed the downstream yard, and access outcomes must be reported with queue costs. The fast layer implements this operational coupling and constitutes the core method. The paired-perturbation tracker remains an optional extension, and its value is judged relative to the fast-only ablation rather than assumed from Eq.~(19).

\section{Conditional mechanism and model-fidelity properties}
\label{sec:analytical-properties}

This section uses two accounting observations and five conditional results to clarify the mechanism; it does not present a solution to the complete terminal control problem. The sequence follows the reader's causal path: appointment uncertainty, gate-to-yard flow, local response information, PTO model fidelity, and safe execution.

\subsection{Structural properties of the gate--yard map}
\label{subsec:5-1-structural-properties-of-the-gate-yard-map}

\noindent\textbf{Observation 1 (confirmation does not determine realized arrivals).} Suppose that

\[
\operatorname{Var}(A_t\mid a_t,H_t,C_t,E_t)
=\sigma_t^2(a_t,H_t,C_t,E_t)>0.
\]

Then realized arrivals are not conditionally deterministic after the current decision and appointment-pipeline state are fixed. In particular, there is no deterministic function of \(a_t\), \(H_t\), \(C_t\), and \(E_t\) that equals \(A_t\) almost surely.

Positive conditional variance rules out a deterministic mapping from the observed confirmation state to physical arrivals. A plan can regulate the arrival distribution, but it cannot eliminate no-shows, timing shifts, exception arrivals, or other compliance variation.

\noindent\textbf{Observation 2 (gate-to-yard workload propagation).} Equation~(2) gives the exact accounting identity

\[
N_{t+1}^y-N_t^y=D_t^g-D_t^y.
\tag{23}
\]

Hence \(D_t^g>D_t^y\) implies \(N_{t+1}^y>N_t^y\). Because actual completions satisfy \(D_t^y\le Y_t(\mu_t^y)\), the stronger condition \(D_t^g>Y_t(\mu_t^y)\) is sufficient, but not necessary, for yard workload to increase.

This accounting result does not imply that every gate-only intervention makes the yard worse than a fixed rule. It says only that realized gate output in excess of realized yard completions increases unfinished yard workload; potential capacity supplies an upper-bound comparison. Because \(I_t^y\) is bounded by the number of active units, persistent linear growth in \(N_t^y=Q_t^y+I_t^y\) also implies persistent growth in the waiting queue. The present evidence cannot isolate the marginal value of joint gate--yard authority because it does not hold the feedback architecture fixed while changing only that authority.

\subsection{Feedback identifiability and conditional queue-drift comparison}
\label{subsec:5-2-feedback-identifiability-and-queue-drift-correction}

For feedback to assign corrective action to the appropriate operational lever, the next observed workload must respond distinguishably to appointment confirmation and yard capacity. Fix a predecision summary \(s\) and define the continuous-relaxation response map

\[
\overline F^{+}(s,\theta)
=\mathbb E[F_{t+1}\mid S_t^{\mathrm{fast}}=s,\theta_t=\theta]
\]

under a locally stationary operating regime. This one-step-ahead definition respects the decision clock: \(F_t\) is observed before \(\theta_t\) is chosen. Let \(F^\star\) be a desired workload target. A balanced point satisfies

\[
\overline F^{+}(s^\star,\theta^\star)=F^\star.
\tag{24}
\]

\noindent\textbf{Proposition 1 (local one-step response identifiability).} Suppose that \(\overline F^{+}(s^\star,\cdot):\mathbb R^2\rightarrow\mathbb R^2\) is continuously differentiable in a neighborhood of an interior continuous-relaxation point \(\theta^\star\) satisfying Eq.~(24), and that its action Jacobian

\[
J_F(\theta^\star)
=\frac{\partial\overline F^{+}(s^\star,\theta)}{\partial\theta^{\mathsf T}}
\bigg|_{\theta=\theta^\star}
\]

is nonsingular. Then there are neighborhoods of \(\theta^\star\) and \(F^\star\) in which the relaxed action producing a given next-window mean feedback vector is locally unique and continuously dependent on that vector.

The proposition gives a precise local interpretation to ``informative feedback.'' If the two actions have indistinguishable next-window effects, the Jacobian is singular or poorly conditioned. The result applies only before the clipping, rounding, and safe-execution operations in Eq.~(18). It neither verifies the hand-set gains in Eq.~(16) nor proves that the implemented rule reduces cost. Appendix~\ref{app:proofs} records the inverse-function argument.

We next state the direct queueing benefit that can be obtained when the feedback signs are operationally valid.

\noindent\textbf{Proposition 2 (conditional reduction in yard-backlog drift).} Fix the same full predecision state \(\Xi_t=\xi\) for two counterfactual actions and evaluate both under the same conditional law of within-window exogenous events. Define the expected realized input--service imbalance

\[
\Delta_t^{\pi}(\xi)
=\mathbb E\!\left[
D_t^{g,\pi}-D_t^{y,\pi}
\mid \Xi_t=\xi,\theta_t=\theta_t^{\pi}
\right],
\qquad \pi\in\{\mathrm{PTO},\mathrm{FB}\}.
\]

Suppose \(\Delta_t^{\mathrm{PTO}}(\xi)>0\) and the feedback action satisfies

\[
\Delta_t^{\mathrm{FB}}(\xi)
\le\Delta_t^{\mathrm{PTO}}(\xi)-\gamma
\]

for some \(\gamma>0\). Then

\[
\mathbb E[N_{t+1}^{y,\mathrm{FB}}-N_t^y\mid \Xi_t=\xi]
\le
\mathbb E[N_{t+1}^{y,\mathrm{PTO}}-N_t^y\mid \Xi_t=\xi]-\gamma.
\tag{25}
\]

If \(\Delta_t^{\mathrm{FB}}(\xi)\le0\), the feedback action removes positive expected unfinished-yard-workload drift for that window.

Conditioning on \(\Xi_t\) fixes the residual-service state for the counterfactual comparison; it does not give the controller information beyond \(S_t^{\mathrm{fast}}\). The condition in Proposition 2 is substantive: the feedback action must reduce upstream pressure, increase realized downstream completions, or do both. Resource and access costs may offset the backlog improvement, so overall performance remains an empirical question. Appendix~\ref{app:proofs} verifies the one-step comparison.

The paired score does not define an estimable stochastic-approximation mean field in the present evidence. Conditional on the information before pair \(n\), its deviation from any chosen reference field can always be partitioned into centered noise and conditional bias, but that accounting identity supplies no convergence argument. Here the bias includes state evolution, fixed perturbation order, changes in fast feedback, adjustment-cost asymmetry, finite perturbations, clipping, and the integer execution mapping. Standard projected stochastic approximation would require additional conditions that are neither imposed nor verified~\citep{KushnerYin2003StochasticApproximation,Borkar2008StochasticApproximation}.

\subsection{Model-fidelity and simulation-selection bounds for PTO}
\label{subsec:5-3-model-fidelity-bounds-for-pto}

Let \(J(u)\) be the expected cost of open-loop plan \(u\) under the true operating environment and \(\widehat J(u)\) its expected cost under the PTO belief model. Define the uniform model-induced cost error over the candidate plan class as

\[
\Delta_{\mathcal U}
=\sup_{u\in\mathcal U}|J(u)-\widehat J(u)|.
\tag{26}
\]

\noindent\textbf{Proposition 3 (exact-model PTO optimality within the open-loop class).} If

\[
J(u)=\widehat J(u),
\qquad\forall u\in\mathcal U,
\]

and

\[
u^{\mathrm{PTO}}\in
\arg\min_{u\in\mathcal U}\widehat J(u),
\]

then

\[
J(u^{\mathrm{PTO}})\le J(u),
\qquad\forall u\in\mathcal U.
\tag{27}
\]

This proposition identifies the population-objective benchmark. It is optimal within the specified open-loop plan class when the planning model reproduces true plan costs. Taking \(\mathcal U=\mathcal U_{\mathrm{pair}}\) gives the ideal fixed-pair specialization, but the implemented benchmark estimates plan costs by finite Monte Carlo simulation and therefore approximates this optimizer. Neither result establishes optimality over the full time-varying class in Eq.~(10) or over closed-loop state-feedback policies outside \(\mathcal U\). Appendix~\ref{app:proofs} records the direct argument.

\noindent\textbf{Proposition 4 (PTO model-fidelity and finite-sample selection bounds).} Let

\[
u^{\mathrm{PTO}}
\in\arg\min_{u\in\mathcal U}\widehat J(u),
\qquad
u^*\in\arg\min_{u\in\mathcal U}J(u),
\]

and suppose \(\Delta_{\mathcal U}<\infty\). Then

\[
J(u^{\mathrm{PTO}})-J(u^*)
\le2\Delta_{\mathcal U}.
\tag{28}
\]

For the finite-simulation selector, let \(\widetilde J_N(u)\) be the Monte Carlo estimate of \(\widehat J(u)\) based on planning sample size \(N\), and let

\[
u_N^{\mathrm{MC}}
\in\arg\min_{u\in\mathcal U}\widetilde J_N(u).
\]

On any event for which

\[
\sup_{u\in\mathcal U}
\left|\widetilde J_N(u)-\widehat J(u)\right|
\le\varepsilon_N,
\]

the selected plan satisfies

\[
J(u_N^{\mathrm{MC}})-J(u^*)
\le2\Delta_{\mathcal U}+2\varepsilon_N.
\tag{28a}
\]

Equations~(28)--(28a) separate model error from finite planning-sample error. In the implemented model-fidelity selector, \(\mathcal U_{\mathrm{pair}}\) contains 95 fixed pairs: 19 integer quotas from 12 through 30 crossed with five integer yard-capacity levels from 6 through 10. Each candidate is evaluated on four common planning paths at each fidelity level. For zero-based scenario, fidelity-level, and planning-sample indices \(s\), \(f\), and \(n\), the model seed is \(70000+10000s+1000f+n\), and the fixed-policy seed is \(91000+n\). Ties are resolved lexicographically by \((\text{estimated mean cost},\text{quota},\text{yard capacity})\). The archive retains all 380 candidate--fidelity records and the selected pairs. No numerical concentration radius \(\varepsilon_N\) is estimated, so Eq.~(28a) remains a conditional deterministic bound rather than a finite-sample confidence guarantee for the reported selector. Appendix~\ref{app:proofs} gives both comparisons.

\noindent\textbf{Proposition 5 (persistent realized completion imbalance under a maintained PTO instruction).} Suppose a fixed-pair PTO instruction, possibly selected under an overestimated yard-capacity model, is maintained during the \(K\)-window comparison without online re-estimation or re-solving from realized completions. If the induced executed actions satisfy

\[
\mathbb E[D_t^{g,\mathrm{PTO}}-D_t^{y,\mathrm{PTO}}]
\ge\delta>0,
\tag{29}
\]

then

\[
\mathbb E[N_{t+1}^y-N_t^y]\ge\delta.
\tag{30}
\]

If the analogous condition holds in each of \(K\) consecutive windows under the maintained instruction, then

\[
\mathbb E[N_{t+K}^y-N_t^y]\ge K\delta.
\tag{31}
\]

Proposition 5 does not derive Eq.~(29) from capacity overestimation. It states the queue consequence once a maintained instruction induces realized gate output above realized yard completions. Because \(D_t^{y,\mathrm{PTO}}\le Y_t(\mu_t^{y,\mathrm{PTO}})\), the stronger condition \(\mathbb E[D_t^{g,\mathrm{PTO}}-Y_t(\mu_t^{y,\mathrm{PTO}})]\ge\delta\) is sufficient, but not necessary, for Eq.~(29). Capacity overestimation is one possible antecedent of this imbalance. Re-identification matters because it may change the executed action and terminate Eq.~(29); the proposition does not claim that lack of re-identification is a logically necessary cause of drift. Appendix~\ref{app:proofs} gives the one-window and cumulative arguments.

\subsection{Safe feasibility and limits of the analytical claim}
\label{subsec:5-4-safe-feasibility-and-limits-of-the-analytical-claim}

The implemented execution map supplies the final operational guardrail. Let \(\Theta_t^{\mathrm{FB},d}\) denote the implementable subset of \(\Theta_t^{\mathrm{FB}}\) after the prescribed integer appointment and discrete-capacity mappings. For any raw physical instruction \(v_t\), the executed feedback action is

\[
\theta_t
=\Gamma_t^{\mathrm{FB}}(v_t)
\in\Theta_t^{\mathrm{FB},d},
\tag{32}
\]

provided that the joint feasible set is nonempty. The map clips the quota and capacity to their physical bounds, applies the pre-specified deterministic integer mapping, and enforces the compatible appointment-access floor in Eq.~(5); current requests then determine \(a_t=\min\{R_t,q_t\}\). Feasibility therefore follows from the construction of the executed map; Eq.~(32) makes no nearest-Euclidean-point claim.

The execution map ensures action feasibility, not queue stability or economic optimality. Those outcomes additionally require sufficient capacity, a valid feedback direction, acceptable resource and adjustment costs, and the appointment-access safeguard. Appendix~\ref{app:proofs} records the membership argument. The analysis consequently supports the following conditional interpretation.

First, the exact-model PTO optimizer is optimal within its specified open-loop class; the model-fidelity Monte Carlo selector inherits that interpretation only up to the finite planning-sample error in Eq.~(28a). The explore-then-select and rolling-surrogate implementations are empirical planning benchmarks rather than instances of that bound. Second, as model fidelity deteriorates, the open-loop guarantee weakens and a maintained instruction can produce positive downstream drift when realized gate output persistently exceeds realized yard completions. Third, a feedback action reduces that drift only when it actually lowers the realized input--service imbalance; resource use and appointment access must still be evaluated. Fourth, local response identifiability does not validate the selected gains, and the paired tracker has no established convergence guarantee.

These boundaries are central to the paper's contribution. The proposed method is valuable not because it defeats perfect predictive planning by assumption, but because port operations are exposed to compliance variation, service-time uncertainty, capacity disruptions, and evolving workload conditions. Feedback coordination supplies a transparent correction mechanism when the realized service ecosystem departs from the model on which the plan was based.

These results determine the order of the computational study. The experiments first compare representatives of the three policy families against the implemented fixed-pair PTO benchmark, then isolate model fidelity and use a separate cadence-triggered recovery diagnostic to compare post-warm-up persistence across trigger times, and finally examine appointment access and design sensitivity. This sequence tests when the restricted high-fidelity PTO benchmark remains strong and when workload-responsive correction can respond to a maintained realized imbalance.

\section{Computational study: public-data-calibrated experiments and mechanism diagnostics}
\label{sec:computational-study}

The computational study separates public-data-calibrated comparisons from independent mechanism diagnostics. The fixed rule represents non-responsive operation. High-fidelity and misspecified fixed-pair PTO represent predictive planning. A separate aggregate reconstruction evaluates delayed activation of a prespecified quota-recovery rule; it is not an adaptive or re-solved PTO policy. The feedback variants are fast-only correction and fast-plus-tracker feedback.

We label the calibration block E0 and the three policy experiments E1--E3. E0 separates calibrated inputs from simulation assumptions. E1 compares the three policy families under high-fidelity information and immediately asks whether the optional tracker improves fast correction. E2 then degrades only the PTO belief and follows the stress test with a separate aggregate reconstruction of recovery timing and feedback-component choice. E3 examines appointment access and the implemented congestion-and-access weighting, followed by an independent gain--lateness diagnostic. Each block has a distinct evidentiary role and data-generating pipeline.

\subsection{Model misspecification and recovery timing}
\label{subsec:6-4-pto-model-fidelity-and-the-value-of-feedback-correction}

E2 asks what happens when a predictive planner maintains an outdated service model near the capacity boundary. The public-data-calibrated peak environment remains fixed; only the belief used by fixed-pair PTO changes. High fidelity uses unbiased request-volume and service-time factors. Mild misspecification multiplies assumed gate and yard service times by 0.90 and 0.88 and the effective request/appearance volume used in planning by 0.95; the corresponding factors are 0.75, 0.70, and 0.92 under medium misspecification and 0.58, 0.52, and 0.90 under severe misspecification. The appearance-probability vector itself is unchanged. The archived Monte Carlo selector chooses \((20,8)\), \((23,8)\), \((28,7)\), and \((30,6)\) under high fidelity, mild, medium, and severe misspecification, respectively, and then evaluates each pair in the unchanged true environment without re-identification. The fast-plus-tracker reference is held fixed and never receives the distorted PTO belief. It starts every replication from the archived physical pair \((25,8)\), equivalently \(z^0=(13/18,1/2)\); the same initializer and feedback settings are used at all four PTO fidelity levels. The experiment does not test sensitivity to alternative feedback initializers.

E2 is a fixed-pair no-reidentification stress test for model maintenance, not a rolling-horizon or certainty-equivalent PTO using the same observations to update and re-solve. The subsequent aggregate diagnostic is motivated by the same downstream-overload mechanism but asks a narrower question about trigger timing, post-warm-up persistence, and component choice. Vehicles still unfinished at the drain limit are excluded from the waiting quantile and reported separately. Under medium and severe misspecification, every replication reaches the two-day drain limit with average unfinished counts of 1,951.58 and 6,448.42 trucks, respectively. The 11,601-minute value therefore indicates an unstable stress regime rather than a normal service level, and exclusion of unfinished vehicles can understate the most severe tail. \section{Discussion and managerial implications}
\label{sec:policy-implications}

\subsection{When predictive planning remains reliable}

The evidence favors predictive planning as the operating backbone when the maintained model ranks feasible plans accurately. In E1, the explore-then-select fixed-pair implementation has the lowest weighted cost and waiting among the reported representatives. In E2, the separate Monte Carlo selector also favors high-fidelity planning; only that selector is subject to the finite planning-sample term in Eq.~(28a). The aggregate reconstruction adds a narrower persistence diagnostic: rules triggered after one or three days are already active before the seven-day evaluation boundary and enter it with little residual backlog. Because this is not a common-state delay comparison and the diagnostic neither estimates a model parameter nor solves an updated plan, it does not report the performance of re-identified or rolling-horizon PTO. The managerial question remains whether model maintenance and feasible action revision occur before congestion builds.

The component evidence also discourages unnecessary algorithmic complexity. In the vehicle-level diagnostic, enabling the paired tracker makes late-horizon drift more negative but produces higher cost, P95 waiting, and unstable-classification shares than fast-only correction. In the separate aggregate reconstruction, fast-only has lower cost, terminal backlog, and drift, and the pointwise seed-block intervals exclude zero. Those intervals quantify simulation-seed variability only and do not turn the reconstruction into an event-matched or vehicle-level comparison. The tracker remains an exploratory option; the simpler fast rule is the parsimonious feedback specification under the reported diagnostics.

\subsection{When workload correction can bridge planning updates}

E2 illustrates a narrower role for within-window correction. Under medium and severe errors, the selected vehicle-level fixed pair induces realized gate output above realized yard completions, and maintaining that instruction allows unresolved workload to accumulate before a revised model is available. Fast-plus-tracker feedback responds to the observed imbalance in the original stress path. In the separate aggregate reconstruction, both feedback specifications reduce the maintained \((24,6)\) reference's late-horizon drift from 5.526 to values within 0.001 trucks per window of zero. The quota-recovery branches triggered on days one and three have post-warm-up diagnostic costs of 0.078 and 0.094, whereas the branch triggered at the day-seven evaluation boundary has cost 5.921. Because the earlier branches receive warm-up recovery time, these are trajectory-persistence results, not from-start cost estimates for three otherwise matched response delays. They describe a documented quota-recovery rule, not periodic parameter re-identification or adaptive PTO.

The tandem model adds an organizational implication. Appointment governance should connect gate access with yard workload, equipment status, exception handling, and downstream completions. Equation~(2) establishes the flow linkage, but the current experiments do not isolate the marginal effect of joint authority because a gate-only and joint policy were not compared under one archived architecture.

\subsection{Information, access, and deployment requirements}

The archived feedback policy has a narrow input requirement: the workload correction uses the gate queue and the yard queue plus busy yard units, while the mean of up to six preceding request counts enters only its access floor. The previous quota enters the realized switching cost observed by the optional tracker, but current requests, outstanding cohorts, recent arrivals, gate-busy count, and current disruptions are not direct workload-correction inputs. A broader deployment may monitor those additional quantities for forecasting, audit, and model maintenance. Operational value also depends on authority: if the appointment book is already closed or yard capacity cannot change at the decision frequency, workload signals can diagnose imbalance but cannot implement the proposed correction.

Waiting must be evaluated together with appointment access. E3 reports lower normalized cost for fixed-pair PTO and higher confirmation for fast-plus-tracker feedback under the tested vectors. Because \(W_1\) and \(W_2\) change a combined congestion-and-access score, they do not identify the standalone causal effect or monetary value of unconfirmed demand. The separate post hoc threshold leaves that external valuation to the decision maker. A deployment should therefore report waiting, throughput, confirmation, unconfirmed demand, resource use, and distributional effects jointly.
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper develops a gate--yard framework in which a terminal confirms new appointment requests and activates yard capacity while previously confirmed cohorts remain immutable. The decision clock, appointment pipeline, service state, and safe execution maps distinguish confirmation from cancellation and planning instructions from executed actions.

The computational evidence supports a conditional operating sequence. Explore-then-select fixed-pair planning is the strongest cost and waiting benchmark in E1, and a separate high-fidelity Monte Carlo-selected pair is strongest in E2. A materially overstated service model can create persistent congestion when it induces a maintained instruction whose realized gate output exceeds realized yard completions. The documented independent aggregate reconstruction shows that trajectories with earlier quota-rule activation enter the post-warm-up evaluation period with much less persistent congestion, but it is not a common-state delay experiment and neither estimates capacity nor re-solves PTO. In its feedback comparison, fast-only correction has lower aggregate diagnostic cost, terminal backlog, and late-horizon drift than fast-plus-tracker feedback, with pointwise seed-block intervals that exclude zero. The archived vehicle-level E2 design contains only fast-plus-tracker feedback, so the aggregate result is not a matched fast-only E2 ablation.

For practice, the resulting interpretation is to plan predictively, monitor both workload and model fidelity, re-estimate and re-solve when those capabilities are available, and use workload correction to bridge replanning latency. The aggregate quota-recovery rule is an illustrative response-timing diagnostic rather than a proposed substitute for adaptive PTO. Any implementation must preserve appointment access, coordinate gate and yard information, and report service and access outcomes jointly.

The principal limitations concern evidence scope rather than loss of the vehicle-level implementation. Aggregate public records discipline the scenarios but do not provide linked vehicle-level appointment--arrival--gate--yard trajectories or identify all terminal-specific parameters. E1--E3 and the high-fidelity component diagnostic are reproducible from the archived source, configurations, deterministic seed rules, execution mappings, and raw outputs. E2 nevertheless contains no matched fast-only arm and does not test alternative feedback initializers.

Tables~\ref{tab:aggregate-recovery} and~\ref{tab:fast-feedback-robustness} are generated by separate documented aggregate reconstructions. Their code, configurations, seeds, and path-level outputs are archived, but their absolute values are not pooled with vehicle-level E2. The seed-block intervals in Table~\ref{tab:aggregate-recovery} cover Monte Carlo and starting-seed variability within that reconstruction, not structural, calibration, parameter, or field uncertainty. Future work should compare fast-only and fast-plus-tracker feedback with adaptive time-varying PTO in one vehicle-level pipeline under bidirectional errors, align response triggers at a common evaluation state, and add a controlled gate-only-versus-joint-authority ablation.

\section{Proofs of the analytical results}
\label{app:proofs}

\subsection*{Proof of Proposition 1}

Continuous differentiability of \(\overline F^{+}(s^\star,\cdot)\) and \(\det J_F(\theta^\star)\ne0\) satisfy the inverse function theorem. The relaxed next-window response map consequently has a continuously differentiable local inverse around \(\theta^\star\), establishing local uniqueness and continuous dependence on the target feedback vector. \(\square\)

\subsection*{Proof of Proposition 2}

For either counterfactual action, Eq.~(2) gives \(N_{t+1}^{y,\pi}-N_t^y=D_t^{g,\pi}-D_t^{y,\pi}\). Taking conditional expectations from the same full state \(\xi\) under the same conditional exogenous law and applying \(\Delta_t^{\mathrm{FB}}(\xi)\le\Delta_t^{\mathrm{PTO}}(\xi)-\gamma\) yields Eq.~(25). \(\square\)

\subsection*{Proof of Proposition 3}

PTO optimality under the belief model gives \(\widehat J(u^{\mathrm{PTO}})\le\widehat J(u)\) for every \(u\in\mathcal U\). When \(\widehat J(u)=J(u)\) throughout this open-loop class, substitution yields Eq.~(27). \(\square\)

\subsection*{Proof of Proposition 4}

The uniform model-error bound and PTO optimality give

\[
J(u^{\mathrm{PTO}})
\le \widehat J(u^{\mathrm{PTO}})+\Delta_{\mathcal U}
\le \widehat J(u^*)+\Delta_{\mathcal U}
\le J(u^*)+2\Delta_{\mathcal U},
\]

which is Eq.~(28). For the finite-sample extension, on the stated uniform-error event,

\[
\begin{aligned}
J(u_N^{\mathrm{MC}})
&\le \widehat J(u_N^{\mathrm{MC}})+\Delta_{\mathcal U}\\
&\le \widetilde J_N(u_N^{\mathrm{MC}})+\Delta_{\mathcal U}+\varepsilon_N\\
&\le \widetilde J_N(u^*)+\Delta_{\mathcal U}+\varepsilon_N\\
&\le \widehat J(u^*)+\Delta_{\mathcal U}+2\varepsilon_N\\
&\le J(u^*)+2\Delta_{\mathcal U}+2\varepsilon_N.
\end{aligned}
\]

This is Eq.~(28a). \(\square\)

\subsection*{Proof of Proposition 5}

Equation~(2) gives \(N_{t+1}^y-N_t^y=D_t^{g,\mathrm{PTO}}-D_t^{y,\mathrm{PTO}}\). Taking expectations and applying Eq.~(29) yields Eq.~(30); summing the same identity over \(K\) windows yields Eq.~(31). In addition, \(D_t^{y,\mathrm{PTO}}\le Y_t(\mu_t^{y,\mathrm{PTO}})\), so the potential-capacity condition stated after Proposition 5 is sufficient for Eq.~(29). \(\square\)

\subsection*{Execution-map feasibility argument}

By construction, \(\Gamma_t^{\mathrm{FB}}\) applies the physical quota and capacity bounds, the pre-specified integer mapping, and the compatible access floor. Whenever \(\Theta_t^{\mathrm{FB},d}\) is nonempty, its output therefore belongs to \(\Theta_t^{\mathrm{FB},d}\), which proves Eq.~(32). This argument establishes membership only and makes no nearest-point claim. \(\square\)
